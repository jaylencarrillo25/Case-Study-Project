Algorithmic Credit Scoring and Discrimination

Jaylen Carrillo, Chenyue Pan, An-zhi Lu, Nicole Gerondidakis

The System Wasn't Built for Everyone

Algorithmic credit scoring affects many parts of life, including loans, housing, insurance, and jobs. These systems use historical data that has been shaped by decades of racial discrimination, which continues to create cycles of inequality for communities of color.

How Credit Scoring Works

Credit scoring systems use your financial data to decide things like loan approvals, interest rates, insurance rates, and even job opportunities. However, the data they rely on is often shaped by existing inequalities. This means your score can reflect more than just your financial behavior.

What comes out: A credit score that affects your ability to get a loan, rent a home, get insured, or find a job.

It's important to note that these scores don't just reflect your actions, they can reflect biased systems and unequal access to financial services.

Marginalized Communities Are the Most Harmed

  • Black and Latino borrowers are pushed into high-cost loans
  • More likely to be denied loans and housing
  • Lower credit scores from using payday lenders or similar services
  • Face more foreclosures and financial setbacks
"African-American and Latino communities will lose $194 billion and $177 billion in housing wealth as a result of the foreclosure crisis." - Center for Responsible Lending

What Needs to Change

  • Use more diverse and fair data when calculating credit scores
  • Hold lenders and financial products accountable, not just the borrowers
  • Make credit scoring systems easier to understand and explain
"Fixing credit scoring is not only a moral imperative, it's a legal obligation." - Rice & Swesnik

Who This Site Is For

This explainer is for students, policymakers, designers, and anyone wondering how fairness gets lost in the data.
